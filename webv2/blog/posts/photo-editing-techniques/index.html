Photo Editing Techniques I Use

Arbin Niroula · 5 min read · 2026

Over time, I've developed a workflow that balances speed with quality. Whether I'm editing a photo for social media or cutting a video for a client, the process is always rooted in the same core principles: efficiency, intent, and consistency.

Step 1: Organise First

Before touching any tool, I organise my images and select the best ones to work on. Culling down to the best shots saves a huge amount of time and means I only invest effort in photos worth keeping.

Desktop Photo Retouching

For detailed retouching on desktop, I rely on Photoshop and CorelDRAW. Photoshop is unmatched for adjusting colors, fixing lighting, removing imperfections, and compositing. Layer masks and selective adjustments let me make precise edits without touching the original pixels.

CorelDRAW comes in for graphic design work — logos, posters, and any vector-based elements that need to scale cleanly.

Mobile Editing

For quick edits on the go, Pixellab is my go-to. It's surprisingly powerful for a mobile app — great for adding text effects, overlays, and quick color corrections. When speed matters, it delivers.

Filters and Style

I experiment with filters and effects to give photos a unique mood, always making sure they align with the project's theme. I avoid over-processing — the goal is to enhance, not to make it obvious that an edit has been made.

Video Editing

For video, I primarily use CapCut and InShot. My focus is always on clean cuts, smooth transitions, and subtle effects that enhance the visuals without overdoing them. Less is almost always more.

Staying Consistent

Consistency is everything. By keeping a standard workflow and saving presets for my most-used adjustments, I maintain a recognisable style across all my photo and video projects. People should be able to look at my work and know it's mine — that's the goal.
